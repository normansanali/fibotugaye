The New America Foundation’s Future Frontlines Project is managed by Candace Rondeaux. She also teaches in the Centre for the Future of War at Arizona State University’s practices division. She is now undertaking research for a book about the Wagner Group and has testified before Congress regarding Russia’s use of paramilitary groups in Ukraine and other countries.

Rondeaux is a Pulitzer Prize-winning author and a former bureau chief for The Washington Post’s South Asia division. This essay is based on a recent New America report on the Wagner Group. She expresses her opinions in this piece. Visit CNN to see more opinions.

The private military firm Wagner Group has frequently been in the vanguard of combat since the beginning of Russia’s full-scale invasion of Ukraine a year ago, and it is still one of the most formidable forces on the battlefield right now.

Its leader, Yevgeny Prigozhin, who describes himself as funding the Russian paramilitary, is rumored to be either a prospective challenger of Russian President Vladimir Putin or a potential assassination target.

Regardless, there are more and more indications that the Wagner Group and Prigozhin are paying a price for their loyalty to Putin and the powerful Russian oligarchs who finance the paramilitary. Despite all the hyperventilating rumors about Prigozhin’s ascension to power, there are still a number of significant concerns that need to be resolved.

How did a country like Russia, which boasts one of the world’s most powerful military and nuclear arsenals, come to rely on the personal army of a millionaire felon who doesn’t appear to have any combat experience?

Could Prigozhin actually replace Putin by using the Wagner Group to gain power? The Wagner Group’s potential impact on upcoming talks between Russia and Ukraine is the biggest worry for the stability of the world.

The resolution of Putin’s conflict and the course of ties between Russia, the US, and NATO will both be greatly influenced by the answers to these issues.
